Vietnam War — Historical Overview
Source: Wikipedia compilation

GENERAL OVERVIEW
The Vietnam War (1 November 1955 – 30 April 1975) was an armed conflict in Vietnam, Laos, and Cambodia fought between North Vietnam and South Vietnam and their allies. North Vietnam was supported by the Soviet Union and China, while South Vietnam was supported by the United States and other anti-communist nations. Direct US military involvement escalated from 1965 until US forces were withdrawn in 1973. The war exacted an enormous cost: estimates of Vietnamese soldiers and civilians killed range from 970,000 to 3 million. 58,220 US service members died.

ESCALATION (1964-1968)
Following the Gulf of Tonkin incident in 1964, the US Congress passed a resolution that gave President Lyndon B. Johnson authority to increase military presence without declaring war. Johnson launched a bombing campaign of the north and deployed combat troops, dramatically increasing deployment to 184,000 by 1966, and 536,000 by 1969. Between 1965 and 1968, Rolling Thunder deluged the north with a million tons of missiles, rockets and bombs.

Johnson pledged he was not "committing American boys to fighting a war that I think ought to be fought by the boys of Asia to help protect their own land."

TET OFFENSIVE — TURNING POINT (1968)
In 1968, the communists launched the Tet Offensive, as over 100 cities were attacked by over 85,000 troops. The offensive constituted an intelligence failure on the scale of Pearl Harbor. During the first month, 1,100 American troops, 2,100 ARVN and 14,000 civilians were killed. Peter Arnett reported an infantry commander saying of the Battle of Bến Tre that "it became necessary to destroy the village in order to save it."

Prior to Tet, Westmoreland had said "the end comes into view." Thus, the public was shocked and confused. Public approval dropped from 48% to 36%, and endorsement for the war fell from 40% to 26%.

Johnson declined to run for re-election. His escalation of the war divided Americans, cost 30,000 American lives by that point and is regarded as having destroyed his presidency. Robert McNamara later said "the dangerous illusion of victory by the United States was therefore dead."

ANTI-WAR MOVEMENT
During the war a large segment of Americans became opposed to US involvement. In January 1967, 32% of Americans thought the US had made a mistake in sending troops. By 1970, 60% believed the US had made a mistake.

Many young people protested because they were being drafted, others because the anti-war movement grew popular among the counterculture. Riots broke out at the 1968 Democratic National Convention. Revelations of the My Lai massacre, in which a US Army unit raped and killed more than 500 Vietnamese civilians, provoked outrage. In 1971, the Pentagon Papers were leaked to The New York Times — the secret history of US involvement detailed public deceptions by the government.

In October 1969, the Vietnam Moratorium attracted millions. The fatal shooting of students at Kent State University in 1970 led to nationwide university protests.

MORALE COLLAPSE OF AMERICAN SOLDIERS
Following Tet, US forces began a period of morale collapse and disobedience. Desertion rates quadrupled from 1966 levels. Drug usage increased — 30% regularly used marijuana, while 10% used heroin. From 1969, search-and-destroy operations became referred to as "search and avoid", falsifying battle reports while avoiding guerrillas. 900 fragging incidents (killing a fellow officer, usually superior) were investigated, most between 1969 and 1971.

Shelby Stanton wrote on the collapse: "Racial incidents, drug abuse, combat disobedience, and crime reflected growing idleness, resentment... An entire American army was sacrificed on the battlefield of Vietnam."

Refusal to engage in patrols emerged, with a case of an entire company refusing orders. A practice known as "sand-bagging" started — units ordered to patrol would find a site out of view from superiors and radio in false coordinates.

PARIS PEACE ACCORDS AND US WITHDRAWAL (1973)
On 27 January 1973, the Paris Peace Accords were signed. This ended direct US involvement in the war. All US forces personnel were withdrawn by March. The Nobel Peace Prize was awarded to Kissinger and Thọ, but Thọ declined saying peace did not yet exist.

FALL OF SAIGON (April 30, 1975)
Chaos and panic broke out as South Vietnamese officials and civilians scrambled to leave. American helicopters began evacuating people from the embassy compound. In the morning of 30 April, the last US Marines evacuated the embassy by helicopter, as civilians swamped the perimeter. PAVN troops entered Saigon and the VC flag was raised above the Independence Palace.

An embittered and tearful President Thiệu had resigned, declaring that the US had betrayed South Vietnam.

CASUALTIES — THE HUMAN COST
- Vietnamese soldiers and civilians killed: 970,000 to 3,000,000
- US military dead: 58,220 (average age: 23)
- South Vietnamese military dead: 254,000+
- Cambodian dead: 275,000–310,000
- Laotian dead: 20,000–62,000
- US wounded: 303,644 (150,000+ seriously)
- US veterans suffering PTSD: ~830,000 (15% of those who served)
- US polling 1978: nearly 72% of Americans believed the war was "fundamentally wrong and immoral"

WAR CRIMES
The 1968 My Lai massacre: a US Army unit raped and killed more than 500 Vietnamese civilians. Torture and ill-treatment were frequently applied to POWs. US records of substantiated war crimes included seven massacres, 78 attacks targeting non-combatants, and 141 cases of torture.

"The Terror of War" by Nick Ut, the 1973 Pulitzer Prize-winning photo, showed a nine-year-old girl running down a road after being severely burned by napalm.

Secretary of Defense McNamara wrote to President Johnson: "The picture of the world's greatest superpower killing or seriously injuring 1,000 noncombatants a week, while trying to pound a tiny backward nation into submission on an issue whose merits are hotly disputed, is not a pretty one."

CHEMICAL WEAPONS AND AGENT ORANGE
Between 1961 and 1971, 20 million gallons of toxic herbicides like Agent Orange were sprayed on 6 million acres of forests and crops. The chemicals continue to cause diseases and birth defects. As of 2006, the Vietnamese government estimated over 4,000,000 victims of dioxin poisoning.

BLACK SOLDIERS' EXPERIENCE
Civil rights leaders protested the disproportionate casualties and overrepresentation in hazardous duty experienced by black servicemen. They endured "the racial insults, cross-burnings and Confederate flags of their white comrades."

REFUGEE CRISIS
Over 3 million people left Vietnam, Laos, and Cambodia after 1975. Between 200,000 and 250,000 boat people died at sea.

CULTURAL IMPACT AND MUSIC
The war influenced a generation of musicians and songwriters. The Vietnam War Song Project identified 5,000+ songs referencing the conflict. Films like The Deer Hunter (1978), Apocalypse Now (1979), Platoon (1986), and Full Metal Jacket (1987) shaped the cultural memory of the war.

THE LONG SHADOW ON AMERICA
US polling in 1978 revealed nearly 72% of Americans believed the war was "fundamentally wrong and immoral." President Reagan coined the term "Vietnam syndrome" to describe the reluctance of the public and politicians to support military interventions abroad.

Kissinger wrote to President Ford: "in terms of military tactics... our armed forces are not suited to this kind of war. Even the Special Forces who had been designed for it could not prevail."

Army Chief of Staff Harold Keith Johnson noted: "if anything came out of Vietnam, it was that air power couldn't do the job."

125,000 Americans left for Canada to avoid the draft, and approximately 50,000 servicemen deserted.
